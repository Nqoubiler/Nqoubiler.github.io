Power BI • Dashboarding • Data Cleaning

Sales Performance Dashboard – Power BI

Objective: To analyse sales data and identify important trends in revenue, monthly performance, category contribution, and profit-related patterns that could support better business planning.

Data Cleaning: I used Power Query to clean and prepare the data before analysis. This included checking and correcting data types, reviewing blanks and errors, standardising date fields, and making sure the dataset was structured correctly for reporting.

How I Did It: I built an interactive dashboard in Power BI using KPI cards, bar charts, line charts, and slicers. I also used DAX and Power Query to support calculations and transform the raw data into a format suitable for analysis.

How I Checked for Accuracy: I compared dashboard totals with the source dataset, checked that calculations matched expected values, reviewed filters and slicers for consistency, and made sure the trends shown in the visuals aligned with the underlying data tables.

What I Was Trying to Find: I wanted to understand which months performed best, whether there were seasonal patterns, which categories were strongest, and where the business might have opportunities to improve performance.

What I Found: December showed peak sales while January and February dropped after the holiday period, indicating seasonality in demand. I also observed differences in category performance and monthly trends, which could help guide promotional timing and stock planning.

How It Helps: Dashboards like this help businesses monitor performance quickly, identify underperforming periods, and make more informed decisions about marketing, inventory, and strategy.

Tools Used: Power BI, Power Query, DAX, Excel

SAS • Biostatistics • Logistic Regression

Cardiovascular Disease Risk Analysis – SAS

Objective: To identify factors associated with cardiovascular disease and determine which variables significantly contributed to a higher likelihood of the event of interest occurring.

Data Cleaning: I reviewed the structure of the data, checked variable formats, prepared categorical and quantitative variables appropriately, and ensured the dataset was suitable for valid statistical analysis before modelling.

How I Did It: I started by examining one-way summaries and selecting relevant explanatory variables. I then performed chi-square tests of association on relevant categorical variables and fitted a logistic regression model that included both categorical and quantitative predictors. After that, I reduced the model by removing non-significant variables and selected the final model using model comparison criteria.

How I Checked for Accuracy: I compared results from the tests of association with the logistic regression findings, performed a goodness-of-fit assessment, checked for overdispersion, and reviewed influential observations to make sure the final model was statistically reliable and appropriately fitted to the data.

What I Was Trying to Find: I wanted to determine which risk factors significantly increased the likelihood of cardiovascular disease and identify the model that best explained the response variable.

What I Found: The final model identified age, serum sodium, and ejection fraction as significant predictors. This showed that both physiological and demographic variables played an important role in understanding cardiovascular risk.

How It Helps the World: This type of analysis can support evidence-based healthcare by helping researchers and health professionals identify higher-risk patients earlier and focus attention on the most meaningful predictors of disease.

Tools Used: SAS, Logistic Regression, Chi-Square Testing, Model Diagnostics

Sampling • Socio-economic Analysis • SAS

General Household Survey Sampling Project

Objective: To estimate the average monthly salary of households in South Africa, the proportion of households earning below the R4500 threshold, and the total monthly salary generated by households whose main source of income is salary.

Data Cleaning: I cleaned the data by removing unrealistic zero values in household monthly salary, removing non-response observations, and using the “Main income source” variable to focus only on households where salary was the main source of income. This improved the quality and relevance of the analysis.

How I Did It: I compared four sampling methods: Simple Random Sampling Without Replacement, Simple Random Sampling With Replacement, Stratified Sampling, and Cluster Sampling. I used these methods to estimate the three population parameters of interest and compare their performance.

How I Checked for Accuracy: I evaluated the precision of each method by comparing confidence intervals for the mean, total, and proportion estimates. The method with the narrower confidence intervals was considered more precise and more reliable for inference.

What I Was Trying to Find: I wanted to determine which sampling method produced the most precise estimates and to better understand income patterns among households in South Africa.

What I Found: Stratified sampling produced the narrowest confidence intervals and emerged as the most effective method. This showed that it provided more precise estimates for average monthly salary, total monthly salary, and the proportion of households earning below the threshold.

How It Helps the World: These findings can support governments, economists, and policy makers by providing insight into income levels, poverty-related vulnerability, and possible tax expectations. This can help guide social support planning and economic policy decisions.

Tools Used: SAS, Sampling Methods, Confidence Intervals, Statistical Inference
